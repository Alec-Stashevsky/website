Recently, I got the opportunity to support some great research with a team of Psychiatrists at the Group for the Advancement of Psychiatry. In the midst of the pandemic lock-downs we sought look for a silver lining and exploited the global transformation of the day-to-day movement of humans.

The Story

We estimated the carbon footprint associated with holding the past two Annual Meetings of the American Psychiatric Association (APA), the largest Psychiatric organization with almost 40,000 members worldwide.1 The unique situation of the COVID-19 pandemic allows us to frame the estimate as a reference for how much carbon emissions were saved by holding the conference virtually in 2020. If you would like to learn about that research, you may read the paper, Wortzel et al. (2021), at JAMA Network Open.

The bottom line is that COVID-19 has radically changed the way humans move through space and this change foreshadows the dramatic behavioral and structural shifts climate change demands from humankind.2 Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions associated with work commutes and industry/professional travel have substantially decreased, leading to distinct improvements in air and water quality (see Yunus, Masago, and Hijioka (2020) and Venter et al. (2020)). Tourism, responsible for 8% of global GHG emissions, has all but stopped (Lenzen et al. (2018)).

This unprecedented reduction in activity has certainly come with many costs, but it is at this crux that such a reassessment can be most enlightening and effective. As we long to return to normal, it is my intent that this research provokes a more thoughtful return. COVID-19 has presented us with an opportunity to rethink the ways we navigate and embody shared spaces, especially when these spaces function as epicenters to grow and build specialized communities whose members are otherwise scattered throughout the world—whether they be centered upon comic books, insurance, SCUBA-diving, recreational vehicles, or mushrooms (a personal favorite).

The carbon footprint of such conferences has been shown to be substantial. Wortzel et al. (2021) estimate the 2018 and 2019 APA Annual Meetings produced about 20 thousand metric tons of carbon-dioxide equivalent emissions each—an amount equivalent to burning 22 million pounds of coal—but moreover, show that this carbon footprint can be reduced by more than two-fold by optimizing the location of the meeting.

The case study in this post is built upon the research of Wortzel et al. (2021), but is by no means limited by context of the APA Annual Meetings. There are hundreds of medical and medical professional associations across the US3 and thousands more industry, professional, hobby, and entertainment focused conventions held yearly. As of 2017, almost 35 million square meters of indoor space was dedicated to some form of exhibition.4 The APA Annual Meeting is by no means a large conference even in the context of medical associations, let alone framed within the total exhibition industry. When we consider the impact of such carbon-conscious planning on an industry-level, the potential reduction in emissions could be considerable.

In this post, I reexamine the ways we navigate to large conferences from around the world and the ways in which the carbon emissions of such conferences can be mitigated while still preserving the important community-building that thrives in these spaces. I expand upon the solutions offered by Wortzel et al. (2021) by analyzing the potential of a regional conference format to mitigate carbon emissions, in addition to the location of the conferences.

A Regional Conference Format

Though regional conferences do not bring whole communities together in one space, there are many aspects that make regional conferences an attractive format, especially if they are held as supplements to large centrally-located conferences.

For one, regional conferences are much smaller and usually break up a community into geographically-connected regions. This smaller format may be easier to administer, less costly, and provide attendees with more intimate opportunities to network with local colleagues. This smaller format may increase engagement in working groups or activities limited by size or participation.

Regional meetings allow for discourse built-up from a more concentrated membership. This may be particularly beneficial for associations advancing policy because such a format allows regions to develop a set of priorities most relevant to them (such as focusing on state or municipal policy) and then advance this smaller set of priorities to a larger base on a national or international scale who may in turn provide much needed support.

For example, US-based medical associations assess and respond to range of policies at the federal and state levels. A regional conference format may allow for discourse that takes place in these 1-5 days to focus more on particularly important state/local regulation which may not appear on the horizon for members outside of the region or district. This format encourages regional members to hone their priorities before larger meetings and present a more concise set of objectives to the entire association rather than having to weigh-in these priorities simultaneously in a larger sea of regulation where they are more likely to go unacknowledged.

Regional meetings may also have the potential to increase total attendance for several reasons. Some members may find it too expensive or too time consuming to travel long distances. This may alienate sections of the member base in fringe geographies and discourage others who feel not enough of their colleagues will attend.

On the other hand, traveling to remote conference destinations can be attractive for those who wish this time to double as a vacation; However, this may leave the conference vulnerable to drops in attendance if locations are cycled across different areas and some are more favorable than others. Such a framework may introduce priorities contradictory to emission reductions as meeting planners may be encouraged to hold meetings in more remote places to encourage attendance significantly increasing the emissions associated with travel.

A Regional Format for the APA

Regional formats can be easy to implement in medical associations or associations involved with advancing policy because there are often existing structures in the administration allowing regions or districts to act independently to a degree. US-based medical associations, like the APA, often have some international base which may or may not be included in the regional structure.5

Simulation Framework

Using attendance data from the APA’s 2018 New York City and 2019 San Francisco Annual Meetings, we assign each attendee to one of seven designated regions based on their origin location and then identify likely transport modes and routes according the assumptions laid out in Wortzel et al. (2021).7

In order to simulate the way attendees would travel to the regional conferences, I had to extend some assumptions from our previous analysis. One critical assumption was how to deal with international (non-Canadian) attendees which are not assigned to any region in the APA’s existing structure, depicted in the map above.8 In this respect we assume international (non-Canadian) attendees will travel to whichever meeting is closest to them. This also ensures our emissions estimation remains conservative.

The second extending assumption was the location of the seven regional meetings. The purpose of the analysis is to quantify the affect of holding multiple smaller regional conferences compared one large one, ceteris paribus. However, in Wortzel et al. (2021) we show that the location of the meeting can have a large impact on the carbon footprint. For this reason, in order to isolate the effect of moving to a regional format and eliminate the noise from different locations, we chose to:

  1. Re-use location of past APA Annual meetings whenever possible.9

  2. Conduct an additional sensitivity test on regional meeting locations allowing all attendees to travel to the closest regional meeting, regardless of their designation.

Bias in Attendance Data

I won’t go into all the implications of why the 2018 and 2019 attendance data sets may be different, but I want to address some inherent biases in the data. Because these data were collected based on the actual attendance of the 2018 and 2019 APA Annual Meetings, we need to consider how this may affect our analysis, and specifically our ability to predict whom will attend what meetings.

Generally, the farther away a conference is, the less likely one is to attend. Traveling farther away is more costly, more time consuming, and it is much easier to drive directly rather than fly if its reasonable. Together these factors influence types of people who attend which meeting. I call this phenomenon geographic bias.

Such bias is present is likely present in the APA attendance data. The 2018 New York City meeting data shows a smaller proportion of members from the West Coast even though approximately 3 thousand more people attended the meeting compared to the 2019 San Francisco one. Similarly, the proportion of attendees from the West Coast increased the the following year at the 2019 meeting.

Though the APA has almost 40 thousand members, only about 13 to 17 thousand attended each meeting. Thus, it is important to have a sense of what factors draw out certain sections of the APA membership and how that could affect our results. It is clear that the meeting’s location exerts influence over who attends the meeting through proximity based on a review of the 2018 and 2019 data; However, there is likely a nexus of other factors that we cannot observe by simply comparing the two data sets and if we were to perform a similar analysis using only one year, it is very likely that our results would be biased towards the people closest to that meeting. We might find that meetings held closer to this critical mass are optimal (in terms of emissions), but we know nothing about what that attendance would look like in an alternate universe where the APA Meeting was held under the beautiful Grand Tetons in, say, Jackson Hole, Wyoming instead of New York City.

In this sense, the analysis would surely benefit from a broader set of attendance data, specifically attendance data from a geographically-diverse set of of conferences. This way, it would be possible to tease out the different sections of the APA’s membership that would have come if the meeting was closer to them.10
